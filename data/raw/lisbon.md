# Lisbon Travel Guide

Lisbon is the capital and largest city of Portugal, known for its hilly terrain,
historic trams, and vibrant neighbourhoods.

## Neighbourhoods

**Alfama** is Lisbon's oldest neighbourhood, a maze of narrow cobblestone streets
tumbling down the hillside below the castle. It is famous for Fado music, the
Castelo de São Jorge, and the Museu Nacional do Azulejo. Great for history lovers.

**Bairro Alto** is a bohemian hilltop district known for its nightlife, independent
shops, and restaurants. By day it is quiet; by night it comes alive with bars and
Fado houses. Budget-friendly food options are everywhere.

**Belém** sits along the river west of the centre and is home to the iconic Torre de
Belém and the Jerónimos Monastery, both UNESCO World Heritage Sites. Don't miss the
famous pastéis de nata from the original Pastéis de Belém bakery.

**Príncipe Real** is an upscale neighbourhood with leafy squares, antique shops,
and some of Lisbon's best restaurants. More relaxed than Bairro Alto, ideal for
a quiet dinner or weekend brunch.

## Food & Drink

Lisbon is a fantastic food city. Must-try dishes include bacalhau (salted cod),
pastéis de nata (custard tarts), and piri-piri chicken. A solid restaurant meal
costs around €15–25 per person. The LX Factory market on Sundays is great for
street food and local produce.

## Getting Around

The city is hilly but very walkable in the centre. Historic trams (especially
Tram 28) are iconic but crowded. The metro is clean and cheap, covering most
major sights. An Uber or taxi from the airport costs around €15–20.
